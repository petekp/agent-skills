# Structural and Stylistic Patterns of AI Writing

Sourced from [Wikipedia: Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing), [WikiProject AI Cleanup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_AI_Cleanup), and [Cherryleaf AI indicators](https://www.cherryleaf.com/2026/02/indicators-that-suggest-something-was-written-by-ai/).

These are patterns in structure, rhythm, and style that distinguish AI writing from human writing. Unlike individual words, these patterns are harder to fix with find-and-replace — they require actual rewriting.

## Structural Tells

### Rule of Three

LLMs compulsively group things in threes:

- Three adjectives: "innovative, dynamic, and transformative"
- Three phrases: "drives growth, fosters innovation, and builds community"
- Three examples per point, three points per section

**Fix:** Vary grouping. Use two items, four items, or one. Not everything needs a list.

### Relentless Balance

Every section gets equal treatment. If there are four topics, each gets a paragraph of near-identical length. Pros and cons appear in perfect symmetry.

**Fix:** Spend more words on what matters more. Real writers show bias — three paragraphs on what fascinates them, one sentence on what doesn't.

### Suspiciously Even Coverage

No section is deeper than any other. Every subtopic receives identical analytical depth regardless of importance or complexity.

**Fix:** Go deep where depth matters. Skim or skip what's obvious.

### Absence of Mess

No false starts, tangents, self-corrections, or digressions. Prose reads as perfectly organized on first pass.

**Fix:** Allow natural editorial messiness. A brief aside, a qualification mid-thought, a sentence that backtracks — these are human.

## Stylistic Tells

### Copula Avoidance

LLMs avoid "is," "are," "was," "has" in favor of elaborate substitutes: "serves as," "stands as," "functions as," "features," "offers," "boasts." One study documented a 10%+ decrease in copula use in AI-influenced academic writing.

**Fix:** Use "is" and "has." Simple verbs are not boring — they're clear.

### Synonym Cycling

Repetition-penalty algorithms cause LLMs to avoid reusing words. A person's name appears once, then they become "the protagonist," "the key figure," "the eponymous character," etc.

**Fix:** Repeat words when repetition is natural. Pronouns and the original term are fine. Forced synonyms are worse than repetition.

### Em-Dash Overuse

LLMs use em dashes (—) far more frequently than human writers, and in places where commas, parentheses, colons, or hyphens would be more natural.

**Fix:** Reserve em dashes for genuine interruptions or asides. Default to commas and periods.

### Excessive Hedging

Constant softening: "often," "typically," "generally," "can be," "may," roughly every third sentence. Nothing is stated definitively.

**Fix:** Make claims. If something is true, say it's true. Hedge only when genuine uncertainty exists.

### Uniform Register

AI maintains one tonal lane throughout — no slippage between formal and colloquial, no accidental self-revelation, no personality leaking through.

**Fix:** Let register shift naturally. A technical explanation can include a casual aside. A formal argument can have a blunt sentence.

### Risk Aversion

AI never writes a potentially confusing sentence, avoids jokes that might not land, avoids claims that might be disputed. The result is "competent, bloodless prose."

**Fix:** Take positions. Make specific claims. Allow an occasional rough edge.

### The Enthusiasm Gap

Every paragraph receives equal investment regardless of subject matter. No section reveals genuine excitement or impatience. Quantum physics and sandwich recipes get the same energy.

**Fix:** Show preference. Spend more energy on what's interesting. Rush through what's not.

### Generic Specificity

Examples feel concrete but name no real companies, people, or events. "Consider a startup that..." / "Imagine a team that..." — plausible-sounding illustrations drawn from genre conventions rather than actual experience.

**Fix:** Use real examples from real experience, or don't use examples at all. Vague hypotheticals are worse than no example.

## Rhetorical Tells

### Perfect Antithesis

Relentless binary oppositions: "Not just X, but Y" / "This isn't about A, it's about B."

**Fix:** Not everything needs a foil. State what something IS without always contrasting what it ISN'T.

### Rhetorical Question Clusters

Clustered fake inquiries: "How do we solve this? What does this mean? Why does this matter?" — three in a row, always answered immediately.

**Fix:** Use rhetorical questions sparingly. One is a device; three is a tic.

### Weasel Attribution

Presenting one source's view as widely held: "scholars agree," "experts have noted," "critics have observed" — while citing one person or no one.

**Fix:** Name the person. "Sarah Chen argues..." is always better than "experts suggest..."

## Formatting Tells

### Title Case Headings

AI defaults to capitalizing every major word in headings. Human writers (outside journalism) tend toward sentence case.

### Mechanical Boldface

Every key term bolded. Every concept name bolded on first appearance. Inherited from readmes, wikis, and slide decks.

### Lists Where Prose Would Work

AI converts flowing arguments into bullet lists, often with each bullet as a complete sentence with period — a visual style from documentation, not natural writing.
